People with Disabilities Are More Likely to Suffer from Long COVID

New CDC data shows people with disabilities are almost twice as likely to get Long COVID as people without disabilities

By Adrian Connor
November 17, 2024

In 2022, the Census’ Household Pulse Survey began including in their monthly survey questions about COVID and Long COVID, or COVID symptoms that last more than three months.

The newly released data, reflecting responses up to September 2024, shows that people with disabilities are consistently more likely to face Long COVID symptoms than non-disabled people.

Valerie Bai, a Family Nurse Practitioner at Penn Presbyterian Medical Center in Philadelphia, said, “People with disabilities like chronic illnesses have compromised immune systems, which increases the likelihood of Long COVID.”

COVID infections also continue to rise among all adults in the United States: in June 2022 40% of adults reported ever having had COVID; by September 2024 nearly 62% of adults reported having had the virus.

Despite the ongoing spread, the CDC relaxed its COVID recommendations in March 2024, treating it more like the flu or a common cold.
